# Copywriting Frameworks — Photography Application Guide

## Hook → Story → Offer
**Use in:** Meta ads, quiz title slides, email sequences, landing page hero
**How:** Hook stops the scroll (under 12 words, specific to genre objection or desire) → Story creates emotional connection (relatable client moment or transformation, 2-4 sentences) → Offer gives the next step (quiz, booking, credit claim). Without the story, you have a hook and a pitch. With it, you have a connection.

## PAS (Problem → Agitate → Solve)
**Use in:** Meta ad primary text, email subjects, landing page problem section
**How:** State the problem in HER words ("You've been putting this off") → twist the knife gently ("And every month your kids get a little older") → present the offer as relief ("One session. One hour. Photos you'll treasure for a lifetime."). Best for short-form.

## The Insight Bridge
**Use in:** Landing page Section 3, Insight Email (#2 in sequence), education-angle ads
**How:** Before you pitch, teach something that reframes the problem. "Here's what most women get wrong about boudoir photography..." → explain the real issue (it's not about being photogenic, it's about having the right guide) → position this studio as the only logical solution. Builds authority. Separates you from studios that just show pretty photos and say "book now."

## BAB (Before → After → Bridge)
**Use in:** Short ads (especially retargeting), follow-up texts, social proof sections
**How:** Paint "before" (1 sentence of her current state) → paint "after" (1 sentence of transformation) → present the studio as bridge (1 sentence + CTA). Total: 3 sentences. Perfect for SMS and retargeting.

Example: "Six months ago, Sarah hadn't been in a photo with her kids in two years. Today, a 30x40 canvas of her whole family hangs above the fireplace. One session made the difference."

## Story Arc Sequencing
**Use in:** Follow-up email sequences (both conversion and nurture tracks)
**How:** Connect messages as a narrative, not standalone pitches. Each email ends with a hook for the next: "Tomorrow I'll share what [client name]'s experience was actually like." Creates serial momentum — open rates on messages 3-7 increase because she wants the next chapter, not just another email.

## The Scroll Test
**Use in:** Positioning statements, landing page headlines, ad hooks
**How:** Read the copy as if it appeared as the first line of a Facebook ad in your feed. Does it stop YOU? Would it stop HER at 11 PM while scrolling? If not, it's too generic. "Capturing life's beautiful moments" fails. "Every woman who booked with us had something she wanted to change first" passes.

## Rule of One
**Use in:** Everything.
**Always:** One reader (specific person in this genre). One big idea (one objection or one transformation). One promise (one benefit). One offer (one next step). If you're trying to serve boudoir clients AND family clients in the same ad, split it.

## The "So What?" Chain
**Use in:** USP development, feature-to-benefit conversion, ad body copy
**How:** For every feature, ask "So what?" twice.
"We have 200 wardrobe pieces" → So what? → "So she doesn't have to stress about what to wear" → So what? → "So the #1 reason women postpone disappears." The second "So what?" is the ad copy.

## 4U Headline Framework
**Use in:** Landing page headlines, email subjects, ad hooks
**Score on 4 dimensions (aim for 3+):**
- **Useful:** Benefit is clear
- **Unique:** Not what every other studio says
- **Urgent:** Reason to act now (time, scarcity, emotion)
- **Ultra-specific:** Number, name, timeframe, or detail

"500+ Women Photographed at Every Size — Your $200 Credit Expires Friday" = all 4.
"Beautiful Photography for Beautiful People" = zero.
