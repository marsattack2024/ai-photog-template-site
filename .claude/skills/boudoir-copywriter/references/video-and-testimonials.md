# Video And Testimonials

## What this file is for
Use this file for UGC scripts, testimonial scripts, talking-head ads, editing guidance, testimonial capture prompts, and any copy that repurposes client stories.

## Why testimonials matter so much in boudoir
In boudoir, trust transfers woman-to-woman.
A nervous buyer is more persuaded by another nervous woman saying “I’m so glad I did it” than by almost any studio-authored claim.
Treat testimonial capture as the center of the creative pipeline, not a bonus.

## The universal testimonial structure
Every strong testimonial should naturally answer:

1. Why did you decide to do this?
2. What was your biggest fear or hesitation?
3. How did it turn out?

These three prompts create the correct emotional arc automatically:
- before
- fear
- experience
- transformation

## Additional probing questions
Use one or two only. Do not over-interview.

- What almost stopped you from booking?
- Were you nervous? What specifically made you nervous?
- How did you feel once you got there?
- What made you feel comfortable?
- What was it like seeing your images for the first time?
- Would you recommend this to another woman? Why?
- Was anything holding you back from booking sooner?

## Themes to steer toward gently
When directing clients or shaping scripts, guide toward these high-converting themes:
- weight, age, or body-confidence concern
- “I almost cancelled”
- “I did this for someone else, but it became something for me”
- “I wanted to celebrate / reclaim myself”
- the huge gap between how scared she was and how fun it was
- what specifically made her comfortable

## Editing rules for all testimonial videos

### Rule 1: Start with emotion, not biography
Do not let the first words be name, hometown, or marital status.
The emotional line must land first.

Good:
- I was so nervous, but the second I walked in…
- I never thought I’d feel this beautiful in photos.
- I almost cancelled my session three times.

Bad:
- Hi, I’m Janet, I’m 42, and I’m from…

### Rule 2: The emotion must hit in the first three seconds
If the viewer feels nothing in the first three seconds, the creative loses force.

### Rule 3: Edit ruthlessly
Cut filler, pleasantries, throat-clearing, and rambling.
A concise, hard-hitting testimonial is more valuable than a long unfocused one.

### Rule 4: Use subtitles and fast emotional payoff
Most viewers consume short-form video silently or semi-silently. Make sure the emotional meaning is visible fast.

## Two ways to deploy testimonial videos as ads

### Format A: Direct transcript -> ad copy
Use the strongest lines from the testimonial itself as headlines or primary text.
The video is the creative. The transcript becomes the copy.

Best for:
- emotionally strong client stories
- body confidence transformations
- “I almost cancelled” stories
- reveal / album reaction clips

### Format B: Written objection-led copy + testimonial visual
Write general ad copy targeting a specific objection, then use the testimonial video as proof.
The written copy attacks the fear. The video validates the promise.

Best for:
- top-of-funnel ads
- retargeting ads
- FAQs in ad form
- when the client video is great emotionally but needs stronger framing

## Core video-ad formats

### 1. Buyer decision questions
Structure:
- hook
- three common questions
- direct answers
- soft CTA

Use when:
- the audience is curious but hesitant
- the unknown is the main blocker

### 2. Versus alternative
Structure:
- compare boudoir to doing nothing / waiting / selfies / spa day / generic gift
- show why this is different
- proof
- CTA

Use when:
- the audience keeps postponing
- you want to make the cost of inaction visible

### 3. Video testimonial / social proof
Structure:
- emotional hook
- fear or before state
- experience
- transformation
- soft CTA

Use when:
- proof is the strongest lever
- you have strong client footage

### 4. How it works / made easy
Structure:
- hook
- step 1
- step 2
- step 3
- result or proof
- CTA

Use when:
- process anxiety is high
- the audience needs demystification

### 5. FAQ / sales questions
Structure:
- scroll-stopping question
- quick answer
- quick answer
- quick answer
- CTA

Use when:
- the audience is deep in consideration
- the questions are practical and repeated

### Bonus reveal / unboxing / partner reaction / green-screen / ASMR formats
These work when the product or reaction provides tangible proof and luxury feeling.
Use them especially for gift angles, product-value angles, and social proof.

## Capture windows
If the user asks for process or operations language, recommend capturing testimonials in these windows:

1. immediately after the session
2. during the reveal / ordering appointment
3. at product pickup

Each window gives a different flavor of emotion and utility.

## How to frame the ask
Ask in a way that centers helping other women, not helping the studio.
Example framing:
“Would you be open to sharing your experience on a quick video? It really helps other women who are nervous about doing this feel less alone.”

## Operational organization logic
When turning testimonials into assets, preserve this flow:
- raw video
- select strongest quotes
- identify objection category
- identify persona and stage
- trim to emotional hook first
- add subtitles
- produce multiple derivative assets

## Derivative asset map
One strong testimonial can become:
- Meta ad primary text
- Meta headline variations
- retargeting ad copy
- website testimonial section copy
- FAQ answers
- email subject lines and body opens
- quiz statements
- social captions
- consultation script proof points

## What not to do
- do not let testimonial copy become generic praise with no fear/transformation detail
- do not keep the opening pleasantries
- do not make every testimonial about the studio; keep the buyer’s feelings central
- do not over-direct the client into polished corporate language
