# Objections And Reframes

## What this file is for
Use this file whenever the copy must overcome hesitation directly: FAQs, sales pages, retargeting ads, consultation pages, phone scripts, nurture emails, DMs, and landing page sections.

## How to use objections correctly
- Do not cram every objection into one short ad.
- Use one objection per short-form asset.
- On long-form pages, sequence objections from most emotionally intense to least.
- Reframe with concrete reassurance, not generic encouragement.
- Whenever possible, answer the objection with process, proof, and control.

## Tier 1 objections: the biggest booking blockers

### 1. “I need to lose weight first.”
What is happening underneath:
- She assumes boudoir is reserved for thin women.
- She thinks the session will document the body she is criticizing.
- She believes future weight loss is the condition for permission.

What to emphasize:
- real women do this at every size
- posing, wardrobe, and lighting change everything
- waiting does not create confidence; action often does
- she is allowed to celebrate the body she has now

Useful phrasing:
- You do not need to lose weight to be photographed beautifully.
- The women you admire in boudoir ads are often everyday women with the same fears.
- Professional posing, lighting, and guidance are designed for real bodies, not perfect ones.

### 2. “What if I look terrible in the photos?”
Underneath:
- fear of regret
- fear that professional photography will confirm her worst self-image

What to emphasize:
- guided posing
- reveal/order process
- only buy what you love if true
- flattering direction and retouching preferences when true

Useful phrasing:
- You do not need to know how to pose. We guide everything.
- You see your images before making final decisions.
- Most clients start exactly here and leave saying they cannot believe that is them.

### 3. “My body has changed. I don’t look like I used to.”
Underneath:
- grief over aging, motherhood, illness, stress, divorce, or weight change
- desire to recover an identity she misses

What to emphasize:
- this is for real-life seasons, not only “best shape” seasons
- boudoir can mark healing, reinvention, and self-return
- the goal is not to erase her life; it is to show her beauty inside it

### 4. “What if people find out?”
Underneath:
- privacy fear
- shame / social judgment fear

What to emphasize:
- written consent policies
- private studio / private reveal
- images never shared without permission if true
- buyer control over wardrobe, posing, and use of images

Useful phrasing:
- Nothing is shared without written consent.
- Your comfort level sets the rules for wardrobe, posing, and privacy.
- Boudoir can be completely private.

### 5. “I’m too old for this.”
Underneath:
- age-coded shame
- fear that boudoir is only for younger women

What to emphasize:
- beauty does not expire
- women across decades do this
- confidence and life experience are assets, not disqualifiers

Useful phrasing:
- Boudoir is not about age. It is about how you want to feel.
- Women in every decade book sessions and say the same thing: I wish I had done it sooner.

## Tier 2 objections: highly common and commercially important

### 6. “I don’t know how to pose.”
Reframe:
- You are not expected to know anything.
- Head-to-toe guidance is part of the service.

### 7. “I have nothing to wear.”
Reframe:
- client closet
- wardrobe consultation
- range from fully clothed to lingerie to implied
- comfort level is hers to set

### 8. “It’s too expensive.”
Reframe:
- frame as investment in how she sees herself
- payment plans if true
- emphasize experience + products + emotional payoff
- avoid sounding defensive

### 9. “What do I do with the photos?”
Reframe:
- albums, wall art, digitals, private keepsakes, partner gifts
- reveal session helps her decide
- many buyers ask this before booking and figure it out once they see the images

### 10. “Will the photographer judge my body?”
Reframe:
- experienced team sees women of every size, age, shape, and story
- emphasis on support, professionalism, and comfort
- all-female team if true

### 11. “What if my partner thinks it’s silly?”
Reframe:
- many sessions begin as gifts and become much more meaningful for the client herself
- partner reactions, when used, should support rather than dominate the self-worth angle

### 12. “What about stretch marks / scars / cellulite?”
Reframe:
- professional lighting, flattering posing, retouching options if true
- discuss preferences openly
- avoid framing the body as a problem to fix

### 13. “What if I don’t like the photos and I’m stuck paying?”
Reframe:
- reveal process
- only buy what you love if true
- transparent expectations and product guidance

### 14. “How naked do I have to be?”
Reframe:
- full control from fully clothed to lingerie to implied or nude if offered
- comfort level is always client-led

### 15. “I’ll look nothing like those women on the website.”
Reframe:
- stress that those are real clients if true
- use “everyday women” proof
- body diversity matters here

## Tier 3 objections: quiet but useful

These are often excellent FAQ prompts, nurture-email topics, quiz questions, and comment-response scripts:
- Who does hair and makeup?
- How long does it take?
- Will anyone else be in the studio?
- I’m not photogenic.
- What if it’s awkward?
- I don’t have a reason to book.
- What if I cry?
- What if I book and chicken out?
- Is this a real studio or a scam?
- My friends will think I’m full of myself.

## Fast reframe bank
Use these in scripts, DMs, or short-form ads when you need direct answers.

- “I’m too old.” -> Women 18 to 70+ book sessions. Confidence does not expire.
- “What if someone sees the photos?” -> Nothing is shared without written consent. Period.
- “I’m not photogenic.” -> That is what almost every client says before seeing her photos.
- “What if it’s awkward?” -> Music, conversation, guided posing, and a team used to helping nervous women. It feels far more natural than people expect.
- “What do I wear?” -> We help with wardrobe from consultation through session.
- “What if I cry?” -> Then you cry. That usually means something real shifted.
- “I don’t have a reason.” -> Wanting to feel like yourself again is a reason.
- “What if I chicken out?” -> Most clients feel that way. Most are grateful they came anyway.

## Where objections belong by asset type

### Ads
- one objection per ad
- if using testimonial video, let the written copy attack the objection and let the video prove it

### Landing pages
- hero or opening section can address the most intense objection or desire
- FAQ handles secondary and tertiary objections
- process section reduces unknowns
- proof section addresses “is this really for someone like me?”

### Emails
- one objection per email
- ideally combine with one client story and one proof point

### Phone scripts / consultation copy
- identify the objection category first
- answer briefly
- move back to the emotional reason she was interested
