# Challenge-style question patterns

Use these as structural patterns, not copy templates.
Do not copy them verbatim into client-facing work unless the wording naturally fits after customization.

## Core patterns

- What is the most important factor in a great [session type] experience?
- Which statement is actually true about [session type]?
- What do most people get wrong about [topic]?
- Do you need to [do something intimidating] before booking?
- What usually makes the biggest difference in a successful [session type] session?
- When is the best time to book or schedule [session type]?
- Which option best describes how [studio name] solves [common objection]?
- What matters more than most people realize during a [session type] session?

## Question design notes

- Prefer one best answer, even if a few choices sound plausible.
- Use wrong answers to reflect common misconceptions, not random nonsense.
- Use answer choices to surface studio benefits naturally.
- Keep the wording simple enough to answer quickly.
- Let the statement slide do the heavier persuasion.

## Genre translation notes

The same structure can work across genres:

- Boudoir: `Do I need to know how to pose before I book?`
- Newborn: `When is the ideal time to photograph a newborn?`
- Maternity: `What usually creates the most flattering maternity images?`
- Branding: `What makes a branding session actually useful for your business?`
- Wedding: `What makes wedding photos feel natural instead of stiff?`
- Headshots: `What creates the strongest professional headshot?`

## Anti-patterns

Avoid using these as the default main-question style:

- What matters most to you?
- Which one sounds best to you?
- How do you feel about your body?
- What kind of experience do you prefer?

Those can work as intake or segmentation prompts, but they usually do not create the `can you get this right` feel.


## Format variation patterns

Use these to vary the rhythm across quizzes without losing the challenge-style feel.

- Standard multiple choice remains the default for most questions.
- A `select all that apply` question works well when several supportive features, reassurances, or benefits are all true.
- A `true or false` question works well for direct myth-busting or privacy and policy clarification.
- An `all of the above` answer can work when the listed options are each real studio strengths and the reveal reinforces value.

## Statement slide voice pattern

- Start with a short bolded headline-style sentence.
- Do not open with `The best answer is` or similarly robotic phrasing.
- Let the headline transition naturally into reassurance, explanation, and persuasion.
- Reveal the right answer in a natural way instead of a quiz-show tone.
